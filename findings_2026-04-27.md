### What the source-code's templates actually fit (verified vs paper + code)

Long-standing assumption: "the source code uses Hamming-weight templates."
**Wrong.** Verified end-to-end against the You & Kuhn paper
(`Single-Trace_Fragement_Template_Attack_on_a_32-Bit_Implementation_of_Keccak.pdf`,
Sect. 2.1) and three concrete files in our pipeline:

**Paper (page 5, "The Template Attack with Stochastic Models")** — quoted verbatim:

> *"When building a template for a target byte b, treat each member bit
> (b[0] to b[7]) as an independent variable, and then use a multivariate
> linear regression to calculate for each point in time coefficients c₀ to
> c₇ and a constant c₈ to predict the expected values of samples as
> x̄_b = Σ_{l=0}^{7} (b[l]·c_l) + c₈, the F₉ stochastic model."*

with **`c_0, …, c_8 ∈ ℝ^m`** (m = number of sample points in the trace).
Each c_l is a *vector*, one entry per sample point — i.e. **per-leakage-point
per-bit coefficients**, not scalars. Then a pooled covariance S is fit and
the runtime classifier is `f(x_a | x̄_b, S) ∝ exp(-½·(x_a-x̄_b)ᵀ S⁻¹ (x_a-x̄_b))`
evaluated for all 256 b.

**Pipeline implementation** — concrete file:line confirmations:

| paper construct | file:line | what's there |
|---|---|---|
| `b[l]` decomposition | `0003_training/template_profiling_bytes/Bits.npy` | shape `(256, 9)` float64; row b is [bit₇, bit₆, …, bit₀, 1]. Verified row 171=0xAB → `[1,0,1,0,1,0,1,1,1]`. The trailing `1` is the intercept. |
| `c_l ∈ ℝ^m` regression | `Template_profiling_round.py:38` | `LinearRegression().fit(InterBits, Re_Traces)` where `InterBits` is N×8 per-byte target bits and `Re_Traces` is N × m LDA-input traces. The fit returns 8 m-dimensional `coef_` rows + one m-dim `intercept_`. |
| LDA dim-reduce | `Template_profiling_round.py:48-65` | W⁻¹·B eigendecomposition; AVectors stored. |
| Per-byte expected projections | `Template_profiling_round.py:72` | `Expects = reg.predict(Bits) * AVectors` → 256 × m' table; saved as `template_expect_bXXX.npy`. |
| Pooled covariance | `Template_profiling_round.py:64-65` | `Scov = AVectorsᵀ · Wmat · AVectors / (Total_Tnum-LDA_DOF)` — single S per byte. |
| Runtime classifier | `0004_validation/template_validation_bytes/Template_validate_LDA.py:81-84` | `Xm = ips_mat * AVE[byte]` (project trace), `matX = ones(256,1)*Xm - EXP[byte]`, `pos = exp(-½·diag(matX·INV·matXᵀ))` — **literally the paper's `f(x_a \| x̄_b, S)` for all 256 b in one go**. |

So: the leakage model the templates assume is the F₉ stochastic model with
**per-sample-point per-bit coefficients**, evaluated as a **256-class shared-
covariance Gaussian classifier** at runtime. HW is a degenerate case (all c_l
collapse to a single direction); a 256-class unconstrained Gaussian (one mean
per byte) needs 256× more training data and is what the F₉ model deliberately
avoids by exploiting the bit structure.

### Where our simulator diverges from F₉

`KeccakSim_BI_TA.py:125`:
```python
self._pbw_byte = pbw_rng.uniform(0.0, 1.0, size=8)   # scalar 8-vec, drawn ONCE
```

The simulator emits `Σᵢ βᵢ·bᵢ` with βᵢ a **scalar** — i.e. `c_l(t) = c_l(t')`
for all sample points t, t'. This is the *collapsed* F₉, where every leakage
point has the same per-bit weight pattern. The paper's c_l are vectors in
ℝ^m; our βᵢ are constants. Templates trained on real silicon (or any source
where c_l(t) genuinely varies across t) see per-PoI structure that our sim
cannot produce.

This is the most likely reason every "leakage richness" intervention has
been a wash:
- widerpbw widens the dynamic range of the *shared* 8-vec — adds variance to
  one weight pattern, not 8 different patterns at different t.
- ICS sweep adds more PoIs but each PoI sees the same bit pattern.
- paperscale adds more traces — templates already converge on the (correct
  for this sim) shared-pattern fit; more traces don't manifest a structure
  the sim isn't generating.

The fix is the canonical F₉: per-sample-point β. That's the advisor's LUT
proposal and it's not an "improvement" on top of pbw — it's the form the
templates were *always* designed for.

### Advisor's per-leakage-point LUT proposal (Apr 29 meeting)

Advisor's framing: precompute, for each leakage point, a 256-entry LUT that
maps `byte_value → leakage_contribution`. The LUT bakes in HW + per-bit
weights at that specific point and stays constant for the entire run.

This is exactly the canonical SLP-2005 stochastic model, just memoised. Two
equivalent representations:

- **Weights form**: store `pbw_byte_per_sample[t, :8]`, draw at init from
  `pbw_rng.uniform(0, 1, size=(T, 8))` where T = trace length. Index by
  `sample_index` inside `_emit_sample_pbw`.
- **LUT form**: precompute `lut[t, b] = β₀(t) + Σᵢ βᵢ(t)·bitᵢ(b)` for all
  256 byte values × T leakage points. Trace generation becomes
  `trace[t] = lut[t, byte_value_at_t] + noise`. Faster than bit
  decomposition + sum at runtime; cleaner.

It IS the standard implementation. Anything different would be a research
deviation. Estimated implementation: ~40 LOC, single new branch
`sim-per-leakpoint-pbw`.

**Status (Apr 29 16:30)**: not implemented yet. Bit-pair branch
(`sim-bit-pair-leakage`) is staged but **deprioritised** — bit-pair adds
quadratic terms on top of a shared-weight linear model that's already wrong;
fixing the linear model first is the cleaner path.

The pattern is real: σ=0.01 wins 0004 mean SR, σ=0.0007 wins SASCA. Pulled

|---|---|---|---|---|

| paperscale_strict | 0.0007 | 9.50% | 910 | low-σ wins 0005 |
| paperscale_widerpbw | 0.0007 | 9.50% | **932** | low-σ + wider pbw wins 0005 |

(One direction matters here: the doc previously phrased this as "less noise →
0004 worse, 0005 better." If recalled in the opposite direction during the
meeting, the data on disk supports the original phrasing.)

**Mechanism (anchor-byte theory, already in § "SIM_NOISE_SIGMA"):** low noise
creates a *bimodal* per-byte SR distribution — a handful of bytes at SR≈1.0
("anchors"), most at SR≈0.005, mean ≈ 9.5%. High noise smears that into a
unimodal mid-range (mean ≈ 10.2% but no SR=1 anchors). SASCA's belief
propagation propagates the SR=1 anchor bytes through Keccak's diffusion as
near-priors; it can do nothing with uniformly mediocre per-byte distributions.
0004's mean-SR metric flattens both regimes into one number that prefers the
unimodal.

**Open question for the leakage-model investigation:** does per-leakage-point
pbw (advisor's LUT) tighten the bimodality further (more anchor bytes) or
flatten it? Templates trained on per-PoI-distinct weight patterns *should*
have more PoIs with high info content for specific byte values, which would
predict more anchors. This is a falsifiable prediction of the LUT fix.

### The missing puzzle piece — why our sim still trails real silicon by ~4× on A

Even with F_9 templates and F_9 sim, **A SR ≈ 6% in sim vs ≈ 36 % real**. Noise
is essentially zero (σ=0.0007 ⇒ R² → 1 at every PoI), the leakage model now
matches what the templates fit, and per-bit BP errors are noise-independent.
So the bottleneck is not noise, not the linear-coefficient form. What's
missing is the **physics** that real silicon adds and our sim doesn't.

Most plausible candidates, ordered by plausibility given what we've ruled out:

1. **Inter-operation Hamming distance (HD = HW(prev_value XOR new_value)).**
   Standard CMOS effect (transitions on bus / register file dominate dynamic
   power). Our sim emits leakage of the **value** computed by each `leak()`
   call — `_emit_sample_byte_lut`, `_emit_sample_pbw`, `_emit_sample` all
   take only the current `value`/`hw`/`byte`, never a previous-value
   reference. Verified: `grep prev_value KeccakSim_BI_TA.py` ⇒ no matches.

   We *did* try `SIM_HD_RATIO=1.0` (`smoke_hd_ics40`, Apr 29) but the
   existing `sim-hd-leakage` branch (commit `60cd0f4`) is
   **substitution-based**, not additive — each `leak()` call uses *either*
   HW *or* HD by random choice (`hd_ratio` = probability), never both. So
   `smoke_hd_ics40` with hd_ratio=1.0 *replaced* HW with HD (A 6.04 %,
   strictly worse). The mixed run `smoke_hd_mixed_ics40` (hd_ratio=0.5)
   was killed by the Apr 29 server restart before completion — but even
   that is **per-byte substitution**, not addition (some bytes leak HW,
   some leak HD; no byte ever leaks both). Real silicon and ELMO/ROSITA
   are **additive**: every sample carries `α·HW(value) + β·HW(value XOR
   prev_value) + noise`. **Additive HW+HD on top of F_9 has not yet been
   tested**; it requires new code (extend F_9 from 9 to 17 parameters with
   per-PoI HD coefficients, or simpler first pass: F_9 + scalar
   `α·HW(value XOR prev)`).

2. **Multi-cycle / multi-sample-per-operation real-chip leakage.** A 32-bit
   ARM op runs through fetch / decode / execute / memory / writeback. Each
   stage's pipeline registers leak. Real traces sample at multiple stages
   per op. Our sim emits exactly one sample per `leak()` (4 per word in
   byte-bitweighted mode, 1 per word in word-bitweighted). The pipeline
   reads ~55 k samples per trace into `Code_detection_R2` either way, but
   in real captures most of those samples are filled by genuine multi-stage
   leakage; in sim they're idle / non-target ops.

   This affects A *most* because the θ + ρ + π chain is the largest stretch
   of in-place memory shuffling per round (load lane, XOR with column, ROL,
   store at permuted index — many register transitions per byte). B/C/D
   are tighter operations where the per-cycle multiplier is smaller. That
   matches the gap: A 36 %/6 % is 6×, B is 5.2 %/3.4 %≈1.5×, D 1.3 %/2 %
   roughly tied.

3. **Microarch fingerprints** — pipeline forwarding, register-file read
   ports, cache line traffic. Untested in any axis. Probably 2nd-order
   relative to (1) and (2).

**What we've already ruled out as the bottleneck:**

- **Coefficient distribution / per-PoI variation (F_9 vs collapsed)** —
  F_9 is the textbook model; our F_9 implementation is bit-exact-correct
  (`_pbw_byte_lut_at` matches `Σ b_i·c_l[i] − ½Σ c_l + c_8` to 0 abs diff).
  The 5-run smoke diagnostic (Apr 30 in flight) tests whether the F_9 <
  collapsed gap at seed=2839 is genuine or seed bias — but **either way,
  flipping the leakage shape inside `leak()` cannot lift A SR from 6 %
  to 36 %**, because both modes already saturate signal at low σ. The
  leakage model captures *what each cycle leaks*; it can't conjure cycles
  that aren't there.
- **Noise level** (4 dp identical across σ ∈ {0.0007, 0.01} for F_9; per-bit
  BP error correlation 0.985).
- **ICS threshold** (ICS sweep null Apr 29).
- **Operand-fetch leakage** (`leakops` branch, Apr 28 ~22:45 — null result;
  added samples were temporally adjacent to result samples and carried
  similar bit-mixes).
- **Pure HD-only leakage** (`smoke_hd_ics40` Apr 29 — but the test
  *replaced* HW; mixed HW+HD never reached completion).

**Cross-check against published Cortex-M leakage simulators** (web research Apr 30):

| Simulator | Year | Target | Models that we don't |
|---|---|---|---|
| ELMO (McCann/Oswald/Whitnall, USENIX) | 2017 | Cortex-M0 | "differential data-dependent effects as neighbouring instructions in a sequence vary" + 3-stage pipeline |
| ROSITA / ELMO* (eprint 2019/1445) | 2019 | Cortex-M0 | direct quote: "loading an operand to the bus leaks the Hamming distance between the value previously held in the bus and the new value"; + pipeline-stage-register transitions |
| M3 micro-architectural power simulator (RG 324400032) | 2018 | Cortex-M3 | pipeline stage registers, memory subsystem transitions |
| ARMISTICE (eprint 2022/467) | 2022 | Cortex-M3 | full microarch leakage model with operand-bus HD |

**Conclusion**: the missing-physics list above isn't speculative — it's just bringing our sim up to the architectural baseline that the published Cortex-M leakage simulators have used since 2017. Our reference paper (You & Kuhn 2022 single-trace fragment template attack on Cortex-M4) targets the **same chip family** ELMO/ROSITA work on; the templates the paper builds against real silicon implicitly absorb HD + pipeline transition effects, but our sim emits only HW of each computed value.

**Recommendations for after the in-flight smoke diagnostic lands:**

- If signed F_9 ≈ unsigned F_9 (almost certainly): coefficient-distribution
  is not the missing piece, ruling out hypothesis (B) in the seed-sweep
  analysis below.
- Then run **additive** HW+HD on top of F_9. **Important caveat:** the
  existing `sim-hd-leakage` branch (commit `60cd0f4`, `SIM_HD_RATIO`) is
  *substitution-based*, not additive — each `leak()` call uses *either*
  HW *or* HD by random choice, never both. That's why the Apr 29
  `smoke_hd_ics40` test (hd_ratio=1.0, pure substitution) gave A=6.04 %:
  it just *replaces* the HW signal, strictly worse. Real silicon (and
  ELMO/ROSITA) is **additive** — every sample carries `α·HW(value) +
  β·HW(value XOR prev_value) + noise`. The right experiment extends F_9
  from 9 parameters (`c_0..c_7, c_8`) to 17 (`c_0..c_7, d_0..d_7, c_8`),
  where `d_i` weights bit `i` of `(byte XOR prev_byte)`. As a simpler
  first pass: F_9 + a single scalar `α·HW(value XOR prev)`. **New branch
  off `sim-per-leakpoint-pbw`, NOT a re-run of `sim-hd-leakage`.**
- If additive-HD-on-F_9 doesn't lift A SR meaningfully, the puzzle moves
  to **per-operation sample density**: sim needs to emit *more* samples
  per Keccak op (e.g. operand-fetch, ALU stage, writeback) — not a wider
  coefficient distribution. ELMO's per-instruction multi-cycle leakage
  model is the conceptual reference here.

### Open question: is F_9 < collapsed real, or seed bias?

The F_9 < collapsed regression at seed=2839 has three plausible explanations
that the existing data can't disentangle:

1. **Seed bias.** Collapsed pbw uses ONE 8-vec for all PoIs; the seed-2839 draw
   `[0.42, 0.43, 0.70, 0.33, 0.60, 0.82, 0.40, 0.46]` (Σw²=2.37) might be a
   "lucky" structure for the templates. F_9 averages over many random per-PoI
   draws so it's less sensitive to seed.
2. **Coefficient distribution mismatch.** Both modes use c_l ∈ U(0,1), all
   non-negative. Real chips have signed coefficients (transitions can decrease
   draw). The U(0,1) restriction may be the bottleneck, not the per-PoI
   variation.
3. **F_9 is intrinsically worse with this pipeline.** The detection
   (`Code_detection_R2/get_corrcoef.py:43`) and template profiling
   (`Template_profiling_round.py:38`) already do per-PoI 8-bit linear
   regression. Adding per-PoI variation to the truth doesn't unlock anything
   the pipeline isn't already exploiting; it just adds variance.

## What each parameter change does (causal attribution)

### `SHA3_TRAINING_ICS_LEVEL` 30 → 90 — dominant gain (~10× on A)

LEVEL=30 keeps every sample with R²(byte_value) > 0.030 → ~1953 features per
byte template. LEVEL=90 keeps R² > 0.090 → ~22 features.

**Mechanism.** LDA-256 fits a 256-class classifier on these features. With
~500 training traces and 1953 features, the within-class scatter matrix is
massively underdetermined. Auto-shrinkage maps the solution toward
identity-covariance; classifier output collapses toward the prior →
SR ≈ 1/256. With 22 features (the *actual* per-bit-weighted emission points),
the LDA problem is well-conditioned and SR jumps to 9–10%.

**Verifier.** Offline pbw probe earlier found those 22 high-R² samples have
`R²_pbw = 0.965` with the 8-bit linear model (vs `R²_HW = 0.470` against the
naive Hamming-weight model). They are, mechanically, the leakage emission
points. Everything else in the LEVEL=30 pool is finite-trace spurious
correlation.

**Open question — sweet spot between 30 and 90?** We jumped 30 → 90 → 120.
30→90 gave ~10× improvement; 90→120 was null (≈ realnoise's numbers).
Untested: 40, 50, 60, 70, 75, 80. **The optimum could plausibly sit at
60-80.** Cost to test: each ICS level requires re-running 0003+0004+0005
(~3-5h smoke scale per level). The 0002 detection already produces all
threshold archives in one pass, so no re-detection needed.

### `SIM_NOISE_SIGMA` 0.0007 vs 0.01 — wins 0004, **loses SASCA**

This knob's effect splits across the two metrics in opposite directions:

- **0004 mean A SR**: σ=0.01 wins (10.0% vs 9.3%). More noise → cleaner ICS
  pool → fewer structurally-bad bytes → uniform per-byte distribution.
- **0005 SASCA recovery**: σ=0.0007 wins (76% / 90% / 90% vs 60% / 86% / 88%
  at 2R/3R/4R). The **bimodal failure mode** that hurts 0004 mean
  (some bytes at SR=0.001) is actually *good for SASCA*: the anchor bytes
  at SR=1.000 act as high-confidence priors that BP propagates through
  Keccak's diffusion. Realnoise's uniform distribution has no anchors and
  the 2R factor graph (less over-determined) suffers most.

**Decision: σ=0.0007 is the right setting** since the goal is SASCA
recovery, not 0004 mean SR. The currently-running paperscale_widerpbw,
paperscale_strict, and leakops_run all use σ=0.0007. paperscale_run
(realnoise) uses σ=0.01 as the deliberate paperscale verification of the
smoke "realnoise loses at SASCA" hypothesis.

### pbw weight range U(0.3,0.7) → U(0,1) — neutral on A/B, -46% D at 0004, **+SASCA**

**What "widerpbw" actually changes.** The per-bit-weighted leakage model
emits `L = β₀ + Σᵢ βᵢ · bitᵢ(byte) + ε`, where each weight `βᵢ` is drawn
once at simulator init. The original `byte_pbw` config drew βᵢ ∈ U(0.3, 0.7)
— all 8 bits contribute roughly equally, so L collapses to ≈ HW(byte) +
small jitter (only ~9 distinct per-byte levels — the Hamming-weight
classes). The `widerpbw` mod opens the range to **βᵢ ∈ U(0, 1)**: some
bits land at weight ≈ 0 (effectively invisible), others at ≈ 1 (binary
contribution). Different bytes with the same HW now produce distinct L
values, pushing the per-byte fingerprint from "9 collapsed classes" toward
"256 unique levels".

This knob splits across metrics:

- **0004**: D-family collapsed from 3.6% to 2.0%. Other families flat.
- **0005 SASCA**: widerpbw is the **best of the three smoke runs** (76 / 94 / 92 vs strict's 76 / 90 / 90).

**Mechanism (0004 side).** Both distributions have mean 0.5; variance goes
0.013 → 0.083 (6×). U(0,1) lets some bits land at weight ≈ 0 (invisible to
the detector). D registers leak through the fewest emission points to
begin with (10 words vs A/B's 50), so they're most fragile to bit-weight
imbalance — hence the D drop.

**Mechanism (SASCA side).** D contributes ~40 byte-priors out of ~200+
total per round; A dominates the factor graph. So D's collapse is mostly
invisible to BP. Meanwhile, the wider weight distribution gives a richer
per-byte mean spectrum at A samples — fewer collisions in the
weighted-bit-sum space → more anchor bytes overall. Net positive at SASCA.

**Confirmed at paper-scale.** paperscale_widerpbw 0004 reproduces smoke
widerpbw's profile (A=0.0951, D=0.0186 — D collapsed exactly as predicted).
Whether it wins at 0005 vs paperscale_strict is still being measured — both
are in 0005 Rate_Scan now. Paperscale_strict has higher D-state SR but
fewer anchor bytes; the SASCA outcome will reveal which matters more.

### `SIM_HW_RATIO` 0.82 → 1.0 — confound, ~1.5× SNR

Removes the byte-independent common-wave additive term. Common-wave is pure
within-class noise → killing it boosts SNR by (1.0/0.82)² ≈ 1.49×. Real but
small. Adopted in all post-baseline runs.

## Why previous runs were "so bad" — bulletproof story

We ran into **two distinct failure modes** that produced superficially
similar results (uniformly weak SR ≈ 1/256, attack basically failed). They
have different mechanisms, different cures, and matter for different
reasons. Both are now diagnosed and reproducible.

### Failure mode A — HW-only leakage simulator (c_scaffold_test, Apr 24)

The pre-bit-weighted simulator emitted **Hamming weight only**:
`sample = center + scale·HW(x) + noise`. For an 8-bit byte, HW(x) ∈ {0,...,8}
takes only 9 distinct values. So no matter how many traces, no matter how
clean the noise model, byte values `x` and `x'` with `HW(x) = HW(x')`
produce **identical mean leakage**. They are mathematically
indistinguishable to a byte-level template.

Consequence: per-byte SR is upper-bounded by `1 / max-class-cardinality
≈ 70/256 ≈ 27%` even in the noise-free limit (the largest HW class is
HW=4, with 70 byte values). In practice with finite traces the LDA fit
collapses well below that — observed: A SR ≈ 1.4% at paper scale.

**Diagnosis chain.** When `c_scaffold_test` (Apr 24, paper-scale env on
HW-only sim) finished with all-byte-weak SASCA, we initially blamed env
parameters. The smoking-gun confirmation came from running the offline
LDA probe on a single byte's IoPs and counting eigenvalues — only 8
non-zero, exactly matching the 9-class HW signal (8 = 9-1 because LDA
loses one dim to the centroid).

**Cure**: the bit-weighted patch (`d078350`, Apr 25) replaces HW(x) with
`Σᵢ wᵢ·bitᵢ(x)` for seeded `wᵢ ∈ U(0,1)` — gives 256 distinct mean
leakages (modulo rare coincidences from random weights) and lifts the
ceiling. **This was the structural fix.** Everything since Apr 25 has
this in place.

### Failure mode B — LDA-256 underdetermined collapse at LEVEL=30 (Apr 25)

After fixing mode A, the first bit-weighted runs (`smoke_byte_pbw`
baseline, Apr 25) still produced SR ≈ 1% on the easiest family. Same
symptom, different mechanism. The bit-weighted simulator was working
correctly — the issue was downstream in the LDA.

**Mechanism, step by step**:

1. The bit-weighted simulator emits leakage **only at specific sample
   positions** in the bit-interleaved Keccak code path — roughly **22
   samples per byte's lifecycle** (load operand, compute shift, write
   back, etc., across one Keccak round). Everywhere else the trace is
   `center + noise`.
2. The 0002 detection step computes per-byte R² with a per-bit linear
   regression at each output sample. At the 22 true emission samples,
   R²_pbw ≈ 0.965 (the linear-in-bits model fits almost perfectly). At
   the rest, R² is dominated by finite-trace random correlations and is
   ~0 in expectation but non-trivially distributed.
3. **`SHA3_TRAINING_ICS_LEVEL=30` selects the ICS archive at threshold
   R² > 0.030** — a very loose threshold. With 64000 trace samples
   per byte and ~1953 random samples whose finite-trace R² happens to
   exceed 0.030 by chance, the IoP archive ends up containing the **22
   real emission samples + ~1931 noise samples**.
4. `0003_training/Code_find_IoPs/get_IoPs.py` builds the IoP archive
   from *all* samples passing the threshold (no further filtering). LDA
   template profiling ingests this as a (~500 training-trace × 1953
   feature) matrix.
5. **LDA-256 on this matrix is underdetermined.** The within-class
   scatter `W` is 1953×1953 with rank at most `Total_Tnum − 256` (degrees
   of freedom = `min(N - n_classes, n_features)`). With ~500 traces ÷
   256 classes ≈ **2 traces per class on average**, the per-class mean
   estimates are noise-dominated, and `W` is near-singular.
6. sklearn's `LinearDiscriminantAnalysis` falls back to **automatic
   shrinkage** (`Ledoit-Wolf` or similar) when `W` is singular — it
   blends `W` with `α·I` (identity) to make it invertible. This pulls
   the discriminant directions toward random. Output: posterior class
   probabilities collapse toward uniform (1/256), and SR ≈ 1/256
   ≈ 0.39%. Observed: ~1% (the easy "outlier" bytes dragging the mean
   up slightly).
7. We were measuring **noise-induced regularization collapse**, not
   template-attack performance. The simulator's leakage was
   exploitable — we just couldn't access it because LDA was drowning in
   spurious features.

**The fix**: `SHA3_TRAINING_ICS_LEVEL=90` selects the R² > 0.090
threshold, which keeps roughly the 22 actual emission samples and
rejects everything else. With 22 features and 500 traces, the LDA
problem is now well-conditioned (~22 traces per feature, well above
the rule-of-thumb 5-10 minimum). SR jumps to 9-10% — what the data
actually supports.

**Why the regression-derived class-mean estimator survives even with 2
traces/class.** The 0003 LDA doesn't use raw per-class empirical means.
It fits a linear regression `L = β₀ + Σᵢ βᵢ·bitᵢ + ε` on (bits, trace)
pairs across **all 64000 traces**, then uses that model to predict the
class mean for any of the 256 byte values. So each `βᵢ` is fit from
~32000 traces (half are bit_i=1), making the predictions far more
robust than naive per-class averaging. This is why the per-byte
template approach works at all in this LDA regime — without it, even
LEVEL=90 wouldn't be enough.

**Why neither more traces nor lower noise rescued mode B.** At LEVEL=30:
- More traces (e.g. paper-scale TS=400 → 64000 traces)? `W` is still
  near-singular because rank is bounded by `min(N − 256, 1953) = 1697` —
  some improvement in conditioning, but the 1931 noise features still
  dominate signal. Predicted ceiling still well below LEVEL=90's.
- Lower noise (σ=0.0007 vs 0.01)? **Counter-productive for mode B.**
  R²-based detection is scale-relative; lower noise means more
  random samples cross the R² > 0.030 threshold (because the
  variance baseline shrinks faster than the signal does). LEVEL=30
  pool gets WORSE at lower σ. This is why the originally-paranoid
  combination σ=0.0007 + LEVEL=30 was specifically the worst regime.

### Reproducibility check

Three quick experiments verify both diagnoses:

1. **Mode A test**: train an LDA on a single byte's IoPs from
   `c_scaffold_test`. Count non-zero eigenvalues — should be ~8. Train
   the same LDA on `smoke_byte_pbw_strict`'s IoPs. Should be ~22-50
   (per-bit-weighted emission produces a richer mean spectrum).
2. **Mode B test**: take any post-Apr-25 sim sandbox and rebuild the
   IoP archive at LEVEL=30 vs LEVEL=90. Run 0003+0004 on both.
   Difference in A SR is ~10×.
3. **Joint test**: σ=0.0007 + LEVEL=30 vs σ=0.0007 + LEVEL=90 on
   the same simulator. SR difference is the upper-bound on what
   ICS-tuning can recover from the noise-induced collapse.

## 0006-0011 are real-data attacks, not part of our sim pipeline

Discovered Apr 28 while validating archive standalone-rerun capability.
The 0006/0007/.../0011 directories assume a different data-source pattern
than 0001-0005. Specifically:

- **0001-0005** are sim-driven. `pipeline_runner/run_full_pipeline.sh`
  invokes `KeccakSim_BI_TA.py` to generate fresh raw traces, then chains
  through preprocessing → detection → training → validation → SASCA.
- **0006-0011** are real-data driven. Each `0006_test_*/SHA3_*_I0X/Raw/`
  has a `script_all.sh` that does literally:

  ```sh
  for i in $(seq -f "%04g" 0 9); do
    wget https://www.cl.cam.ac.uk/research/security/datasets/sha3-32bit/Data/0006_test_*/SHA3_*_I0X/Raw/Raw_*_${i}.zip
  done
  ```

  No simulator hook. The Code_preprocessing/ scripts then run trace QC
  against the reference trace and stash data into Code_preprocessing/
  zips that the template_attack scripts unzip.
- **`run_full_pipeline.sh` has no 0006-0011 phase**. We have never run
  any 0006+ attack in this pipeline. The 4 paperscale sandboxes all have
  empty `0006_test_SHA3_512/SHA3_512_I0X/Raw/` directories.

**Why this matters for archives:** the smoke widerpbw archive has
sim-trained templates. Our `archive_run.sh --include-all` bundles
templates + ICS + Bit_Tables + sim + ref_trace, which is enough to
re-run 0001-0005 from scratch. **It is not enough to run 0006**, because
0006 needs raw silicon traces that come from the Cambridge dataset.

### Can we use Cambridge real traces with sim-trained templates?

In principle yes — that's the natural follow-up experiment. The 0006
attack scripts don't *care* whether the templates came from sim or real
data; they consume `templateLDA_OXXX.zip` from 0003 plus `ics_original_XXX.zip`
from 0002 and apply Mahalanobis scoring. So:

1. Download Cambridge real raw traces into `0006_*/SHA3_*_I0X/Raw/`.
2. Run `0006_*/SHA3_*_I0X/Code_preprocessing/script_all.sh` (computes
   correlation against `0001/Code_reference/ref_trace.npy`, extracts
   I/O bytes, stashes Processed_HDF5/Invocation_*.hdf5).
3. Run `0006_*/SHA3_*_I0X/template_attack_*_NR_I0X/script_all.sh` —
   this is the 0006 attack, using whatever templates `init.sh` unzips.

The unknown: **does a sim-trained template generalize to real silicon
traces?** Two reasons it might fail:

- **Different sample positions matter**. Sim's high-R² emission samples
  are at specific cycles in our simulator's trace timeline. Real silicon
  has its own (sparser, narrower) high-R² positions. ICS detected on sim
  may not align with real silicon's hot points → templates query the
  wrong samples.
- **Different leakage mix at hot samples**. Sim's per-bit weighted
  emission is `Σᵢ wᵢ·bitᵢ` with random `wᵢ ∈ U(0,1)`. Real silicon's
  bit-mix at any given cycle depends on the instruction (whether a
  byte is being loaded, XORed, stored, etc.) and on physical register
  layout. These are unlikely to match the simulator's random-weighted
  decomposition exactly → Mahalanobis scoring against sim-trained
  per-class means will be biased.

Two reasons it might work:

- **Both worlds use linear-in-bits regression to estimate per-class
  means**. The 0003 template profiling fits a linear model in bit
  features and uses it as the class-mean estimator. So even if the
  weights differ between sim and real, the *shape* of the per-class
  mean spectrum (256 means in an LDA subspace) should retain enough
  byte-discriminating signal to be useful.
- **The real-data benchmark itself uses the same byte-LDA framework**.
  Real silicon's templates work on real traces (we have 35.7% A SR).
  If our sim templates approximate real silicon's bit-mix even loosely,
  they should produce some non-trivial discrimination on real traces.

This would be a one-shot experiment: download Cambridge data, run
preprocessing + a 1R attack, see if SR is in the 1-10% range (would
indicate non-trivial template transferability) or 1/256 baseline (would
indicate complete domain mismatch). The cost is a download + ~1 hour
compute.

If it succeeds, our sim-trained templates would be the first known
example of "domain-transferred" SCA templates from sim to real. That's
publication-grade. If it fails, the failure mode (at which BP step does
recovery fall apart) tells us exactly which simulator aspect needs
realism upgrades.

## Operand-leakage hypothesis (sim-leak-operands experiment)

**Diagnosis.** The widening gap to real-data at higher rounds (2R 71% → 3R
61% → 4R 58%) implies the simulator's templates extract less information
per sample point than real silicon's, so each extra round of BP yields less
gain in sim than in real. Real silicon leaks at ~5000-15000 sample points
per Keccak-f permutation (every CPU cycle of every register touch); our
sim emits at ~few hundred (one per result of leak_xor / leak_and / leak_not
/ leak_ROL32).

**Patch.** Each leaky logic primitive now also emits its operands' leakage
(modeling load + load + write-back instead of just the result write):

```python
def leak_xor(self, a, b):
    self.leak(a)        # load a
    self.leak(b)        # load b
    res = self.leak(a ^ b)   # write a^b
    return res
```

Sample count per Keccak-f[1600] under byte-bitweighted: 55296 → 115776
(2.09×). Env trace lengths bumped accordingly in `.env_smoke_leakops`
(every `*_TRACE_LEN` and `*_OUTPUT_SIZE` is 115776). All other
parameters identical to smoke widerpbw — clean A/B test.

**Expected if it works.** SASCA Rate_Scan thresholds at smoke leakops should
exceed smoke widerpbw's iteration-scan-implied levels, and the
rounds-scaling gap (2R vs 4R) should narrow.

**Hypothesis invalidated.** Operand leakage adds samples that are

samples; they nearly triple the trace length without tripling the
information content. Possibly slightly hurt via:
- LDA conditioning: more candidate ICS samples at fixed R² threshold →
  redundancy → LDA picks up spurious correlations between adjacent samples
- R² threshold dynamics: with denser leakage, more samples cross R² > 0.09
  by chance, diluting the IoP pool

**Refined diagnosis**: the rounds-scaling gap to real-data is NOT a
sample-count problem. It's an **information-content-per-sample** problem.
Real silicon's hot samples carry HD (transition) information that our
HW-only sim entirely misses. That's the smoke_hd experiment (next section).

## Hamming-distance leakage hypothesis (sim-hd-leakage experiment)

**Why HD next.** Operand leakage was the wrong fix for the rounds-scaling
gap. CMOS dynamic power consumption is dominated by **bit transitions**
(Hamming distance between consecutive values on a wire/register), not raw
HW. That's textbook physics — Mangard, Oswald, Popp 2007 — and yet our
sim has never modeled it. We've been emitting HW-only leakage as a
stand-in, accepting whatever the linear-bit decomposition could capture.

**Patch (`sim-hd-leakage` branch, commit `60cd0f4`).** New env knob
`SIM_HD_RATIO ∈ [0,1]`:
- 0.0 (default): pure HW emission, identical to previous behaviour.
- 1.0: every leak() emits leakage of (value XOR previous_value) instead
  of value. Per-byte and per-word `prev` tracking — 4 prev_bytes per
  byte position 0-3 in a 32-bit word, plus 1 prev_word for word-level
  granularity.
- Mixed: per-leak-call random switch with probability hd_ratio.

The first few HD samples will look HW-like (since HD vs initial 0 = HW)
but quickly match steady-state HD distribution as the program state
evolves.

**Smoke env**: `.env_smoke_hd` = identical to `.env_smoke_byte_pbw_widerpbw`
except `SIM_HD_RATIO=1.0`. Pure HD on top of widerpbw config (LEVEL=90,
σ=0.0007, U(0,1) weights). Clean A/B test against smoke widerpbw.

**Predicted effect.** If real silicon's information advantage comes from
HD leakage (which is the standard CMOS assumption), then HD-emitting sim
should:
- Lift 0004 A SR (each sample now depends on 2 bytes' bit-patterns
  instead of 1 → richer per-class mean spectrum → fewer collisions)
- Close the 4R rounds-scaling gap (more bit-info per sample = more
  cross-round constraints for BP)
- Possibly hit the 1/256 byte-LDA structural cap less often (HD bytes
  span the full 0..255 range with their own collision pattern,
  different from HW)

**Status**: launched Apr 29 00:31, smoke scale. Headline experiment for
the simulator-fidelity question.

## Can we hit 100% on simulated data?

**Plausibly yes — with per-bit stochastic templates.** Argument:

1. The `byte-bitweighted` simulator's emission is *exactly*
   `L = β₀ + Σᵢ βᵢ·bitᵢ(x) + ε`, ε ~ N(0, σ²), σ = 0.0007 - 0.01.
2. The Bayes-optimal classifier for that leakage form is per-bit linear
   regression: fit β at each (sample, bit) on training data, score test
   traces by Σₛ −(trace[s] − β₀(s) − Σᵢ βᵢ(s)·bitᵢ(x))² / (2σ²(s)).
3. Per-bit per-sample log-likelihood-ratio scales as (β / σ)². With
   β ∈ U(0, 1) and σ = 0.01: peak (0.95 / 0.01)² ≈ 9000 per bit per emission
   sample. Aggregating over ~22 emission samples → log-LR per bit ≈ 200000
   → bit recovery is essentially deterministic.
4. Deterministic bit recovery → byte SR ≈ 100% → SASCA outcome ≈ 100% (no
   prior left for BP to fail on).

**Why LDA-256 doesn't get there.** It fits a 256-class quadratic decision
boundary with no structural assumption. With ~500 training traces and 256
classes (~2 per class), class means are noisy and shrinkage regularization
saturates. Stochastic templates exploit the model structure: 8 bit-level
fits, each effectively trained on the full 500 traces.

**Operand leakage as alternative path.** Adding more sample points per byte
(idea E, sim-leak-operands branch) is an orthogonal way to lift the per-byte
LDA toward the asymptote: with 2× more emission samples, log-LR per bit
roughly doubles. Won't hit 100% on its own but could close the rounds-scaling
gap that currently caps SASCA convergence.

### Closing the A SR gap (sim 9.5% vs real 35.7%) — the core question

The diagnosis from "Failure mode B" + the operand-leakage and ICS-sweep
nulls converged on this picture: the gap is about **information per
sample**, not sample count, not template configuration, not ICS density.
Per-byte LDA-256 templates have a structural cap around 9-10% A SR for
our current emission model — and we've validated that cap by sweeping
every knob *except* leakage content.

Hypotheses for breaking the cap, ordered by ease × expected gain (post-HD-rejection):

| # | Hypothesis | Code touch | Why it might help | Status |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| ~~B~~ | ~~HD leakage~~ | done | (current XOR prev) was supposed to enrich per-byte signal | **NEGATIVE Apr 29 13:37 — destroys signal, not enriches it** |
| **D** | **bit-pair interaction terms** (`L = β₀ + Σβᵢbᵢ + Σβᵢⱼbᵢbⱼ`) | ~30 LOC in `_emit_sample_pbw` | adds 28 cross-coefs per byte; richer fingerprint *within a single sample* (no cross-time-step spreading like HD did) | **not tried — leading candidate** |
| 3 | **per-byte microarch fingerprint** | ~10 LOC + lookup | mimics real silicon's "byte = unique pipeline state" effect; biggest expected gain but speculative | not tried |
| 5 | **position-asymmetric leakage** (byte 0/1/2/3 of a 32-bit word emit differently) | ~10 LOC | real silicon has bus-layout asymmetry; sim treats all 4 byte slots identically | not tried |
| Q | **QDA instead of LDA** | swap in `Template_profiling_round.py` | per-class covariance fit; needs more training traces; orthogonal to sim improvements | not tried |

Why **adding samples doesn't help**:
- ICS sweep null (50/70/90 identical at 4 dp) ⇒ sample density is not the bottleneck.
- Operand-leakage null ⇒ adding leak emission points doesn't help if each one carries the same per-bit linear info.

What "information per sample" looks like in real silicon (and not in our sim):
- ~~HD (transition power)~~ — **tested, negative.** HD spreads byte signal across timesteps in a way our per-sample-R²+LDA pipeline can't recover.
- Bit-pair interactions (β cross-terms in the linear model) — easy to add, **now the leading candidate**. Adds 28 cross-coefficients per byte, all expressed *within a single sample* (no time-step smearing).
- Microarch fingerprint (byte-value-specific cache/pipeline behavior) — biggest potential, hardest to model.
- Position asymmetry (bus layout, register file slot dependence) — small but additive.

**LDA model mismatch is _not_ the simulator-side bottleneck**: sim emission is exactly Gaussian-per-byte by construction, so LDA is the matched classifier on sim. Switching to QDA on sim would add capacity without changing the information ceiling. Real silicon's LDA mismatch (multimodal/heteroscedastic leakage) doesn't *hurt* real templates because real has so much signal that even a wrong-model classifier hits 35%. So Q is a parallel investigation, not on the critical path for the sim-fidelity question.

**Is LDA underdetermined currently?** Yes, but it's not the cap.

| Run | n_features per `(tag,i)` | traces/class | LDA regime |
|---|---|---|---|
| smoke widerpbw, ICS=90 | ~30 | 50×10×16/256 ≈ **31** | underdetermined ⇒ Ledoit-Wolf shrinkage activates |
| smoke widerpbw, ICS=50 | ~46 | ~31 | underdetermined |
| paperscale_widerpbw, ICS=90 | ~30 | 400×10×16/256 ≈ **250** | borderline well-determined |

Two natural underdetermination tests, both already run:

1. **Smoke → paperscale at widerpbw, fixed ICS=90** (8× more traces per class): A SR moved 9.39% → 9.51%, within noise. 8× more training traces produced no gain.
2. **Smoke widerpbw, ICS sweep 50/70/90** (more features per class at fixed trace count): all three give 9.39% A SR bit-identical at 4 dp.

Both tests would have shown movement if LDA fit (rather than leakage content) were the bottleneck. Neither did. Sklearn's LDA with eigen-solver + Ledoit-Wolf does the right thing in low-trace regimes — it pushes Sw toward identity, which on sim is **near-optimal** since sim emission is homoscedastic Gaussian by construction. The classifier isn't misfitting; it's classifying as well as the leakage allows.

**Where underdetermination would matter.** If we add bit-pair interactions
(`Σᵢⱼ βᵢⱼ bᵢ bⱼ`) and want to exploit the new per-class non-Gaussian
moments, we'd need either many more training traces (paperscale-or-bigger)
*or* to switch to QDA. Current LDA-with-shrinkage would silently absorb
the new info into a too-stiff covariance estimate. That's the moment to
revisit Q. Until then, fix the leakage first — the cap is information,
not fit.

## Key learnings consolidated

1. **The original "paper-scale on simulator" run (`c_scaffold_test`) failed
   primarily because the simulator emitted Hamming-weight leakage**, not
   because the env was wrong. Bit-weighted patch was the structural fix.
2. **ICS_LEVEL=30 was diluting the IoP pool** with ~1900 spurious-correlation
   samples per byte, leaving LDA-256 underdetermined. LEVEL=90 fixes it.
   **Sweet spot in 60-80 not yet tested.**
3. **0004 mean SR is a misleading proxy for SASCA outcome.** Optimize for
   SASCA, not 0004 mean. The bimodal anchor-byte effect is what BP needs.
4. **The "more noise is better" smoke-scale conclusion was incomplete.**
   σ=0.01 wins 0004 mean but loses SASCA. σ=0.0007 wins SASCA. Confirmed
   at paperscale_strict + paperscale_widerpbw.
5. **Widening per-bit weights to U(0,1) hurts D-family at 0004 but helps
   SASCA**. Confirmed at paperscale_widerpbw 0004; SASCA partial confirms
   widerpbw is the leading paperscale variant.
6. **The gap to real-data WIDENS with rounds** (2R→4R: 70%→58%). This was
   the signal that motivated the operand-leakage experiment.
7. **Operand leakage is NOT the answer.** Doubling sample density via
   operand-fetch leakage (sim-leak-operands branch) did not close the
   rounds-scaling gap; it slightly hurt iteration_scan max% at 2R/3R.
   The added samples are temporally adjacent to result-leakage samples
   and carry similar bit-mixes — redundant, not complementary.
8. **HD leakage is the right next hypothesis** — but as **additive**, not
   substitutional. Real silicon's per-sample info advantage comes from CMOS
   transition power (HD = value XOR previous_value) **in addition** to
   absolute-value leakage, both contributing to every sample. Our HW-only
   sim entirely misses HD; the existing `sim-hd-leakage` branch tests
   *substitution* (HW or HD per call, never both), which is a strictly
   different model and was rejected Apr 29 13:37. **Additive HW+HD on top
   of F_9 has not yet been tested** — it requires a new branch off
   `sim-per-leakpoint-pbw` extending F_9 from 9 to 17 parameters per PoI
   (8 HW coefs + 8 HD coefs + intercept). This matches what published
   Cortex-M leakage simulators (ELMO 2017, ROSITA / ELMO* 2019, ARMISTICE
   2022) actually model. See "The missing puzzle piece" section earlier
   in this doc.
7. **Real-data 3R/4R Rate_Scan exists**, contradicting an earlier readme
   claim that "only 2R was captured". Full curves now archived locally.
8. **Scale alone (TS=50→400) doesn't move 0004**. Scale matters for 0005
   statistics (more attack traces = denser rate-scan resolution) but not
   for per-byte template SR.

**v2 F9+noise overshoots real silicon by ~2.8× on A-rounds** (99.5% vs 35.8%). The per-position F9 leakage is "too clean" — the templates are perfectly matched to what the simulator emits, producing near-perfect byte classification. Real silicon's 35.8% reflects a mix of HW + HD + multi-cycle pipeline effects that smears the per-bit fingerprint into something harder to classify.

**v2 HW+noise and v2 HD+noise both sit far below real silicon** (~4% vs 36%), similar to the old v1 F9 runs (~2.6–2.9%) in absolute terms. The additive HD (v2 hd mode) did not improve over pure HW here, in contrast to the legacy-HD result (15.4%) in Phase 13. The difference: Phase 13 used the **legacy collapsed-pbw** base model (one shared 8-vec) where HD added genuinely new information, while v2 HW already has per-position weights so the HD term is noisier and less orthogonal.

**Old-sim F9 is the weakest of all**, confirming those earlier runs were hampered by the v1 simulator's architectural issues (multi-cycle + PPC downsampling artifacts on top of already-weak F9 templates).

### Key conclusions and updated outlook

1. **v2 F9 is an upper bound, not a calibration target.** Near-perfect self-consistent leakage gives near-perfect attack, confirming the pipeline is architecturally sound. Not physically realistic.

2. **v2 HW+noise and v2 HD+noise represent the "honest" current simulator** for the HW and additive-HD models. Both sit at ~4% A SR — essentially unchanged from the pre-v2 per-leakpoint F9 baseline (~6%), and far from real silicon (36%).

3. **The additive HD benefit seen in Phase 13 (legacy HD, 15.4%) does not carry into v2.** In v2, adding HD on top of per-position HW weights does not help, presumably because the per-position HW already captures much of the transition-related variation. The Phase 13 result depended on the collapsed (shared-weight) base model providing a clean HW baseline that HD could enrich orthogonally.

4. **Updated A-round SR ordering:**
   - v2 F9+noise (99.5%) >> Real silicon (35.8%) >> legacy HD 0.5 (15.4%) > legacy HD 1.0 (12.8%) > smoke widerpbw (9.4%) > F9 per-leakpoint v1 (6.1%) > v2 HW+noise (~4.4%) ≈ v2 HD+noise (~4.1%) >> old-sim F9 multi/combined (~2.6–2.9%)

5. **The gap between v2 HW/HD (~4%) and real silicon (36%) remains open.** The missing physics is likely multi-cycle per-instruction pipeline leakage (ELMO/ROSITA style): real silicon leaks at every pipeline stage of every instruction, while our simulator emits one sample per `leak()` call. The 9× SR gap (4% vs 36%) is consistent with the ~9× sample-density gap documented in the Phase 15 real-vs-sim trace comparison.

6. **Next lever: ELMO-style multi-cycle emission per operation.** Each Keccak `leak()` should emit samples at fetch, decode, execute, and writeback stages, with appropriate HD between consecutive pipeline register values. This would increase samples per byte substantially and introduce genuine instruction-stream temporal structure. A simpler first pass: emit 3–4 samples per `leak()` with appropriate within-instruction HD (operand-to-result and result-to-result transitions).
